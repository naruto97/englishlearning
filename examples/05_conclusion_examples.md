# Section 5: Conclusion - Comprehensive Examples

## RUBRIC REQUIREMENTS (Exact Language)

**Distinguished (15 Points):**
> Conclusion paragraph exceeds 3 sentences, provides a thoughtful and reflective summary that reinforces the thesis, and brings together all the points addressed in the essay. New details are not added.

**Proficient (12 Points):**
> Conclusion paragraph exceeds 3 sentences, provides reflection that reinforces the thesis, and no new details are added.

**Basic (9 Points):**
> Conclusion paragraph has at least 3 sentences but contains only general reflection that reinforces the thesis but does not bring back all the points addressed in the essay. New details are not added.

**Below Expectations (6 Points):**
> Conclusion paragraph has at least 3 sentences but lacks clear reflection that reinforces the thesis or brings back all the points addressed in the essay or includes new details.

**Non-Performance (3 Points):**
> Conclusion paragraph is fewer than 3 sentences lacking enough detail to provide adequate closure.

---

## POINT BREAKDOWN: What Separates Each Level

| Component | 15 pts (Distinguished) | 12 pts (Proficient) | 9 pts (Basic) | 6 pts (Below) | 3 pts (Non-Perf) |
|-----------|------------------------|---------------------|---------------|---------------|------------------|
| Sentence Count | Exceeds 3 (4+) | Exceeds 3 (4+) | At least 3 | At least 3 | Fewer than 3 |
| Summary | Thoughtful and reflective | Provides reflection | General reflection | Lacks clear reflection | No reflection |
| Thesis | Reinforces | Reinforces | Reinforces | May not reinforce | Not addressed |
| Points Covered | ALL points brought together | Not required | Not all points | Not all points | N/A |
| New Details | None added | None added | None added | May add new details | N/A |

**Key Insight:** The critical difference between 12 and 15 points:
- **15 points:** "brings together ALL THE POINTS addressed in the essay"
- **12 points:** Only requires "reflection that reinforces the thesis"

To earn Distinguished, you MUST reference all three body paragraph arguments in your conclusion.

---

## THE FOUR COMPONENTS OF A DISTINGUISHED CONCLUSION

### Component 1: Restated Thesis (Different Words)

**Purpose:** Remind reader of your main argument without repeating verbatim.

**DO:**
- Rephrase the core argument in fresh words
- Show what you've PROVEN, not just stated
- Maintain the same position

**DON'T:**
- Copy your introduction thesis exactly
- Use completely different language that changes meaning
- Weaken your position

---

### Component 2: Summary of ALL Main Points

**Purpose:** Remind reader of every argument you made (not just one or two).

**DO:**
- Mention ALL THREE body paragraph topics
- Use brief references, not full explanations
- Connect them as parts of a whole

**DON'T:**
- Only mention one or two arguments
- Go into detail again
- Miss any body paragraph topic

---

### Component 3: Thoughtful/Reflective Element

**Purpose:** Show deeper thinking beyond simple summary.

**DO:**
- Acknowledge nuance
- Consider broader implications
- Add perspective on what the evidence means

**DON'T:**
- Just restate facts
- Undermine your argument
- Be superficial

---

### Component 4: Final Thought (Memorable Close)

**Purpose:** Leave the reader with something lasting.

**DO:**
- End with impact
- Connect to broader significance
- Look forward or provide insight

**DON'T:**
- Trail off weakly
- Introduce new arguments
- End with "In conclusion" (overused)

---

## WHAT TO DO: Distinguished-Level Conclusions

### Example 1: Complete Distinguished Conclusion

> "The evidence demonstrates conclusively that for most students, pursuing a college degree represents the wisest investment in their professional futures. [RESTATED THESIS - new words, same position] With substantially higher lifetime earnings, access to a broader range of career opportunities, and the flexibility to adapt as industries evolve, college graduates are positioned for long-term success in ways that trade school and immediate employment cannot replicate. [SUMMARY OF ALL 3 POINTS - earnings, opportunities, flexibility] While alternative paths may suit individuals with specific vocational goals and circumstances, they rarely provide the same breadth of opportunity or financial security over a full career. [THOUGHTFUL REFLECTION - nuanced acknowledgment] The data on earnings, unemployment, and career mobility all point to the same conclusion: higher education pays dividends that compound over decades. [REINFORCEMENT] High school seniors facing this decision should recognize that college is not merely preparation for a first job—it is the foundation for a lifetime of professional growth and financial stability. [FINAL THOUGHT - memorable, forward-looking]"

**Analysis:**
- Sentence count: 5 ✓
- Restated thesis: ✓ ("wisest investment" vs. original "strongest path")
- All 3 points: ✓ (earnings, opportunities, flexibility/adaptability)
- Thoughtful reflection: ✓ (acknowledges alternative paths)
- No new details: ✓
- Memorable final thought: ✓

---

### Example 2: Alternative Distinguished Conclusion

> "When weighing the immediate appeal of trade work against the long-term trajectory of college graduates, the evidence consistently favors higher education. [TRANSITION + RESTATED THESIS] College graduates enjoy not only substantially higher earnings throughout their careers but also greater flexibility to change fields and stronger protection against unemployment during economic downturns. [SUMMARY OF ALL 3 POINTS] Although the upfront investment of time and money is significant, the return—measured in both financial security and professional opportunity—justifies the cost many times over. [REFLECTION - addresses cost concern] For the vast majority of high school graduates, the path to long-term success runs through a college campus. [FINAL THOUGHT - memorable closing image]"

**Analysis:**
- Sentence count: 4 ✓
- All 3 points referenced: ✓
- Reflective element: ✓
- No new information: ✓
- Strong closing: ✓

---

### Example 3: Third Distinguished Conclusion

> "Across every meaningful measure—earnings, career options, job security, adaptability—college graduates outperform their peers who chose alternative paths. [RESTATED THESIS with emphasis] The financial advantage alone, exceeding a million dollars over a lifetime, would justify the investment; combined with greater career mobility and resilience during economic uncertainty, the case for college becomes overwhelming. [SUMMARY OF ALL POINTS] These advantages do not diminish over time but compound, creating a widening gap between those with and without degrees. [REFLECTION - temporal perspective] While trade work and immediate employment offer valid paths for some, they cannot match the breadth of opportunity that college unlocks. [ACKNOWLEDGMENT] For students with the ability and resources to pursue higher education, doing so remains the most reliable route to financial security and professional fulfillment. [FINAL THOUGHT]"

**Analysis:**
- Sentence count: 5 ✓
- Opens with strong synthesis
- References all arguments
- Adds reflective element about compounding advantages
- Ends with clear recommendation

## WHAT NOT TO DO: Conclusion Errors

### Mistake 1: Introducing New Arguments

**Bad Example:**
> "In conclusion, college provides higher earnings and job security. Additionally, the social connections made in college often lead to valuable networking opportunities and lifelong friendships that can open doors throughout one's career."

*Why it fails:*
- "Social connections" and "networking" weren't discussed in body
- This is NEW INFORMATION
- Violates "no new details" requirement
- Belongs in body paragraphs, not conclusion

**How to Fix:**
Either add a body paragraph about networking, OR remove this from the conclusion:
> "In conclusion, college provides the higher earnings, career flexibility, and job security discussed throughout this essay. These advantages combine to make higher education the stronger choice for most graduates."

---

### Mistake 2: Only Mentioning Some Points (Not All)

**Bad Example:**
> "College is clearly the better choice for high school graduates. The earnings data proves that college graduates make significantly more money over their lifetimes. For these reasons, students should choose college."

*Why it fails:*
- Only mentions earnings (Body ¶1)
- Doesn't mention flexibility (Body ¶2)
- Doesn't mention counterargument points (Body ¶3)
- This is 9 points (Basic), not 15

**How to Fix:**
> "College is clearly the better choice for high school graduates. Higher lifetime earnings, greater career flexibility, and the ability to weather economic uncertainty all favor degree holders. While trade work offers short-term advantages, the long-term evidence overwhelmingly supports college education."

Now all three body paragraph topics are referenced.

---

### Mistake 3: Exact Repetition of Introduction Thesis

**Bad Example:**

*Introduction thesis:*
> "College education remains the strongest path for most high school graduates because it provides greater career flexibility, significantly higher lifetime earnings, and critical thinking skills essential for adapting to an evolving economy."

*Conclusion:*
> "As stated earlier, college education remains the strongest path for most high school graduates because it provides greater career flexibility, significantly higher lifetime earnings, and critical thinking skills essential for adapting to an evolving economy."

*Why it fails:*
- Literally the same sentence with "As stated earlier" added
- Shows no development of thought
- Reads as lazy, repetitive
- Conclusion should show what you've PROVEN, not just repeat claims

**How to Fix:**
> "The evidence confirms that college provides advantages its alternatives cannot match. Higher earnings, broader career options, and adaptable skills combine to position graduates for lifelong professional success."

Same meaning, different words, shows synthesis.

---

### Mistake 4: Undermining Your Argument

**Bad Example:**
> "Of course, this is just my personal opinion, and everyone should do what's best for them. College isn't for everyone, and there's nothing wrong with other paths."

*Why it fails:*
- "Just my personal opinion" undermines entire essay
- "Nothing wrong with other paths" contradicts thesis
- Weakens the argument you've built
- Thesis should be based on evidence, not opinion

**How to Fix:**
> "While individual circumstances may vary, the evidence strongly supports college as the optimal path for most students. The advantages documented throughout this essay—financial, professional, and personal—combine to make higher education a wise investment."

Acknowledges nuance without undermining position.

---

### Mistake 5: The "In Conclusion" Cliché Opening

**Bad Example:**
> "In conclusion, college is better than trade school or working. To sum up, college provides many benefits."

*Why it fails:*
- "In conclusion" is overused and weak
- "To sum up" is redundant after "In conclusion"
- Signals amateur writing
- Reads like a checklist

**Better Alternatives:**

Instead of "In conclusion," try:
- "The evidence demonstrates..."
- "When weighing the options..."
- "Across every measure..."
- "These findings confirm..."
- Just start with your restated thesis

**How to Fix:**
> "The evidence consistently demonstrates that college provides advantages trade work and immediate employment cannot match. Graduates benefit from higher earnings, greater flexibility, and stronger job security throughout their careers."

---

### Mistake 6: Too Brief (Inadequate Closure)

**Bad Example:**
> "College is better. Students should go to college. The end."

*Why it fails:*
- Only 3 sentences (minimum, not exceeding)
- No reflection or depth
- "The end" is childish
- Provides no closure or summary

**How to Fix:**
Add substance and remove "The end":
> "College remains the stronger choice for most high school graduates. With higher earnings, greater career flexibility, and better job security, degree holders outperform their peers across every significant measure. While the initial investment is substantial, the returns—financial and professional—justify the cost many times over. High school seniors should view college not as a delay but as a foundation for lasting success."

---

### Mistake 7: Adding New Evidence or Citations

**Bad Example:**
> "In conclusion, college is the better path. In fact, a 2024 Harvard study found that college graduates report 40% higher life satisfaction than non-graduates (Harvard)."

*Why it fails:*
- The Harvard study is NEW INFORMATION
- Citations belong in body paragraphs
- Conclusion summarizes; it doesn't introduce
- Violates "no new details" rule

**How to Fix:**
Either add this study to a body paragraph, OR remove it from conclusion:
> "In conclusion, college graduates enjoy advantages in earnings, career options, and overall life satisfaction. The evidence presented throughout this essay supports higher education as the wisest investment for most students."

## THE "ALL POINTS" REQUIREMENT

### How to Reference All Three Body Paragraph Topics

Your conclusion must touch on each body paragraph's main argument. Here's how:

**Body Paragraphs Were About:**
1. Higher lifetime earnings
2. Career flexibility
3. Counterargument (trade benefits are real but limited)

**One-Sentence Summary (All Three):**
> "Higher lifetime earnings, broader career options, and the ability to adapt as industries change all favor the college-educated worker, while the short-term advantages of trade work diminish over time."

This single sentence touches:
- Earnings (Body ¶1) ✓
- Career options/flexibility (Body ¶2) ✓
- Trade work advantages diminish (Body ¶3 counterargument) ✓

---

### Examples of Summary Sentences That Cover All Points

**Example A:**
> "With substantially higher earnings, greater career mobility, and stronger resilience during economic downturns—advantages that trade work cannot match long-term—college graduates are positioned for lifelong success."

**Example B:**
> "The data on earnings, unemployment, career flexibility, and long-term growth all point to the same conclusion: despite trade school's practical appeal, college delivers returns that compound over decades."

**Example C:**
> "While trade workers may earn more initially, college graduates' advantages in lifetime earnings, career options, and adaptability ultimately prove decisive."

## REFLECTIVE ELEMENTS: Going Beyond Summary

### What Makes Reflection "Thoughtful"

**Thoughtful reflection:**
- Considers broader implications
- Acknowledges complexity
- Shows synthesis of evidence
- Provides insight beyond facts

**General reflection (loses points):**
- Just restates findings
- Doesn't add perspective
- Superficial summary
- No depth of thought

---

### Examples of Thoughtful Reflection

**Example A (Acknowledging Nuance):**
> "While alternative paths may suit individuals with specific vocational goals and circumstances, they rarely provide the same breadth of opportunity or financial security over a full career."

*Why it's thoughtful:* Acknowledges exceptions without undermining thesis.

**Example B (Broader Implication):**
> "These advantages do not merely persist—they compound over time, creating a widening gap between those with and without degrees that has significant implications for economic inequality."

*Why it's thoughtful:* Connects to larger social issue.

**Example C (Temporal Perspective):**
> "The decision at eighteen thus casts a long shadow, shaping not just one's first job but one's earning potential, career trajectory, and financial security for the next four decades."

*Why it's thoughtful:* Emphasizes long-term significance.

## FINAL THOUGHT OPTIONS

### Type 1: Forward-Looking Statement

> "High school seniors facing this crossroads should recognize that college is not a delay in starting life but the foundation upon which lasting professional success is built."

### Type 2: Call to Action

> "For students with the opportunity to pursue higher education, the evidence strongly supports seizing that opportunity."

### Type 3: Broader Significance

> "In an economy increasingly divided between knowledge workers and service workers, the choice of whether to attend college may determine which side of that divide one occupies for life."

### Type 4: Memorable Image/Metaphor

> "The four years invested in higher education are not a pause before real life begins—they are the launchpad for it."

### Type 5: Return to Hook

> "Of those 3.7 million high school seniors facing this decision each May, those who choose college will, on average, be better off forty years later by every measure that matters."

## COMPLETE CONCLUSION COMPARISON

### Distinguished (15/15)

> "The evidence demonstrates conclusively that for most students, pursuing a college degree represents the wisest investment in their professional futures. With substantially higher lifetime earnings, access to a broader range of career opportunities, and the flexibility to adapt as industries evolve, college graduates are positioned for long-term success in ways that trade school and immediate employment cannot replicate. While alternative paths may suit individuals with specific vocational goals, they rarely provide the same breadth of opportunity or financial security over a full career. High school seniors facing this decision should recognize that college is not merely preparation for a first job—it is the foundation for a lifetime of professional growth and financial stability."

- 4+ sentences ✓
- All 3 points referenced ✓
- Thoughtful reflection ✓
- No new information ✓
- Memorable final thought ✓

---

### Proficient (12/15)

> "College is clearly the better choice for most high school graduates. The evidence shows that college graduates earn more money, have more career options, and enjoy greater job security. While trade school has its benefits, college provides advantages that last a lifetime. Students should seriously consider attending college after high school."

*Why 12 instead of 15:*
- Has 4 sentences ✓
- References points but less thoroughly
- Reflection is adequate but not "thoughtful"
- Final thought is weak ("should seriously consider")

---

### Basic (9/15)

> "College is a good choice for students. College graduates make more money than people who don't go to college. This is why students should go to college."

*Why 9:*
- Only 3 sentences (meets minimum but doesn't exceed)
- Only references ONE point (money)
- Missing flexibility and counterargument points
- No reflection
- No memorable closing

---

### Below Expectations (6/15)

> "College and trade school are both good options. Students should think about what they want to do. Going to college costs money, but it can help you get a job."

*Why 6:*
- Fails to reinforce thesis clearly ("both good options" is not a position)
- Introduces cost concern (potentially new detail)
- Doesn't bring back all points
- Vague and unfocused

---

### Non-Performance (3/15)

> "College is important. Go to college."

*Why 3:*
- Only 2 sentences
- No reflection
- No closure
- Completely inadequate

## SELF-CHECK QUESTIONS

1. **Sentence Count:** Do I have at least 4 sentences in my conclusion?

2. **Thesis Check:** Have I restated my thesis in NEW words (not copied from intro)?

3. **All Points Test:** Can I identify where I reference Body ¶1, Body ¶2, AND Body ¶3?

4. **New Info Test:** Have I introduced any statistics, evidence, or arguments not already in my body paragraphs?

5. **Reflection Test:** Have I included a sentence that shows deeper thinking beyond simple summary?

6. **Closing Test:** Does my final sentence feel memorable and conclusive?

7. **Undermining Test:** Have I accidentally weakened my argument with phrases like "just my opinion"?
